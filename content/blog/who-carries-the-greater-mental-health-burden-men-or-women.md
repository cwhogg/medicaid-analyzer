Women report worse mental health than men at every age, in every year on record. That's not the surprise. The surprise is how much worse things have gotten for young women specifically, and how far the gap has stretched since 2016.

## The Overall Gap Has Widened by Nearly 2 Points Since 2016

Start with the national picture. In 2016, **29.3% of women** reported 14 or more poor mental health days in the past 30, compared to 21.7% of men. That's a 7.6 percentage point gap. By 2024, women hit 37.7% and men 28.5%, pushing the gap to 9.2 points.

Both sexes got worse. Men climbed 6.8 points over eight years. Women climbed 8.4 points. The crisis is real for everyone, but women are pulling further ahead in the wrong direction.

The 2020-to-2023 jump is particularly stark. Men went from 22.7% to 28.1%, a 5.4-point rise. Women went from 33.1% to 36.9%, adding 3.8 points. Men actually closed the gap slightly in that window, but by 2024 the gap had widened again to its largest recorded level.

## Young Women Are Carrying a Disproportionate Share

Age breaks this story open. Across all age groups, the gender gap runs between 7 and 11 points, but the absolute rates at the top of the age distribution are dramatically lower than at the bottom.

| Age Group | Male % | Female % | Gap |
|-----------|--------|----------|-----|
| 18-24 | 40.0 | 51.5 | 11.4 |
| 25-34 | 32.9 | 42.3 | 9.4 |
| 35-44 | 26.8 | 36.7 | 10.0 |
| 45-54 | 21.8 | 33.2 | 11.4 |
| 55-64 | 18.0 | 28.0 | 10.1 |
| 65+ | 12.4 | 19.7 | 7.4 |

More than half of women aged 18-24 report frequent poor mental health days. That's not a marginal finding. **51.5% of young women** clearing the 14-day threshold means frequent mental distress is the statistical norm for that group, not the exception.

The 45-54 bracket ties 18-24 for the largest gender gap at 11.4 points, but the absolute rates are far lower. Young adults are in a different category entirely.

## Among Young Adults, the Gap Has Grown Faster Than Anywhere Else

The trend data for 18-to-24-year-olds is the most alarming piece of this analysis. In 2016, 44.0% of young women reported frequent poor mental health days versus 35.5% of young men, an 8.5-point gap. By 2024, young women hit 57.3% and young men 44.9%, stretching the gap to 12.4 points.

Young men aged 18-24 rose 9.4 points over that period. Young women rose 13.3 points. Both trajectories are bad. Women's is worse.

For context, the 25-34 group started 2016 with a smaller gap: 35.8% for women versus 27.7% for men, a difference of 8.1 points. The 18-24 gap started higher and has grown faster.

**By 2024, more than 4 in 10 young men** and more than 5 in 10 young women were reporting at least two weeks of poor mental health in a given month. At the other end of the age spectrum, men 65 and older came in at 12.4%. The distance between those two numbers, both male, is larger than the entire female rate for seniors.

The gender gap in mental health is real, persistent, and widening. But the more urgent story may be what's happening to young adults of both sexes, with young women now past the majority threshold and still climbing.
