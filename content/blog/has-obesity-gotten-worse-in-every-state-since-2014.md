## In 2014, Six States Had Obesity Rates Below 25%. None Do Anymore.

Every state that once sat below the 25% obesity threshold has now crossed it — and the least-affected states in the country today are simply yesterday's least-affected states, now several points heavier. The floor has risen everywhere.

## Key Numbers

- **Colorado**, the leanest state in 2014 at **21.3%**, now sits at **25.0%** — a **3.7-point increase** and still the lowest obesity rate in the country in 2024
- **DC** climbed from **21.7%** to **25.5%**, ranking second-lowest nationally in 2024
- **Hawaii** and **Massachusetts** both reached **27.0%** by 2024, up from **22.1%** and **23.3%** respectively — tied for third-lowest
- **Vermont** rose **4.2 points** to **29.0%**, the largest absolute increase among the six formerly sub-25% states
- **California** went from **24.7%** to **29.1%**, ranking sixth-lowest nationally despite nearly hitting the 30% mark

## The Former Elite Still Lead — But the Gap Has Closed

The six states that sat below 25% in 2014 still occupy the bottom six ranks in 2024. The ordering has barely shuffled. Colorado leads, DC follows, Hawaii and Massachusetts are tied, Vermont is fifth, California sixth. A decade of change, and the relative hierarchy is nearly identical.

What has changed is the absolute level. In 2014, Colorado's 21.3% sat nearly 4 points clear of the 25% threshold. Today it sits exactly at that threshold. The states that once represented a distinct low-obesity tier have converged toward the national pack, even as they retain their relative positions.

| State | 2014 Obesity % | 2024 Obesity % | Change (pts) | 2024 Rank (Low→High) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Colorado | 21.3 | 25.0 | +3.7 | 1 |
| DC | 21.7 | 25.5 | +3.8 | 2 |

| Massachusetts | 23.3 | 27.0 | +3.7 | 3 |

| California | 24.7 | 29.1 | +4.4 | 6 |

Vermont and California — the two states closest to the 25% line in 2014 — both now approach 29–30%, a range that would have placed them in the middle of the national distribution a decade ago.

## The 25% Threshold Is Now a Floor, Not a Ceiling

In 2014, 25% functioned as a ceiling that only six states stayed beneath. By 2024, it's effectively the national floor — no state sits below it. The entire distribution has shifted upward.

The smallest increase among these six states was **3.7 percentage points**, shared by Colorado and Massachusetts. The largest was Hawaii's **4.9-point** climb. That's a tight band: every formerly sub-25% state added between 3.7 and 4.9 points over the decade, regardless of starting position or geography. Colorado (Mountain West), Hawaii (Pacific), Massachusetts (Northeast), Vermont (Northeast), California (Pacific), and DC (Mid-Atlantic) span radically different demographic and policy environments — yet all moved in the same direction by roughly the same magnitude.

California's trajectory is worth isolating. At 24.7% in 2014, it was the closest of the six to the threshold. At 29.1% in 2024, it now ranks sixth-lowest nationally — meaning it has retained its relative standing even as it nearly hit 30%. The state added **4.4 points** while still outperforming most of the country.

## Open Questions

1. If the six historically leanest states all increased by 3.7–4.9 points over the same decade, are the states that started higher in 2014 showing similar uniform increases — or has the distribution widened, with higher-obesity states accelerating faster?

2. Colorado and Massachusetts both added exactly 3.7 points despite being geographically, demographically, and politically distinct — is that convergence in the rate of increase a statistical artifact of BRFSS methodology, or does it reflect something structural about how obesity spreads across already-low-prevalence populations?

3. Given that no state remains below 25% as of 2024, what threshold — if any — now meaningfully separates the low end of the distribution from the rest?
